Where the World Drinks Safely — SDG 6.1.1

This project models global inequalities in clean water access by mapping the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal indicator 6.1.1—the percentage of a population using a safely managed drinking water service. The workflow began in QGIS, where I joined national-level water data to country boundary shapefiles, filtered out tracking errors, and standardized the results.

The interactive version was built using Leaflet.js, incorporating custom styling, dynamic pop-ups, and an interactive legend to display global inequalities cleanly.

Neuroscience Connection: Cognitive Load Optimization

Human visual search relies heavily on pre-attentive feature processing. By grouping data into a simplified, clear 5-class choropleth layout, this map aligns with the brain's working memory constraints (Cowan’s K-factor), letting users immediately spot global patterns without experiencing cognitive exhaustion from messy visuals.

Kindergarten Accessibility in Utrecht by Bicycle

Spatial Question: How accessible are early childhood educational facilities across the municipality of Utrecht when using active bicycle transport, and where do infrastructural inequities occur?

Methods & Steps: I built a network dataset using real cycling pathways rather than simple straight-line buffers. Using vector network service area tools, I generated travel-time polygons (isochrones) at intervals of 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, and 24 minutes. I then ran a Vector → Analysis → Count Points in Polygon process, setting the isochrones as the base layer to calculate the exact number of kindergartens accessible within each time threshold across the municipal zones.

Findings & Spatial Patterns: The data revealed strong clustering within the urban core, where families can access multiple facilities in under 8 minutes. However, peripheral residential sectors showed distinct "service deserts," requiring over 20 minutes of travel time.

Neuroscience Connection: Spatial Mapping & Child Development

Early childhood mobility is closely linked to the development of place cells and grid cells in the hippocampal formation. Children who easily explore rich, varied urban spaces build complex neural representations of spatial layout. This means that gaps in infrastructure can directly influence how young minds learn to map and interact with their surroundings.

Flood Risk & Spatial Vulnerability Analysis

Spatial Question: Which urban catchment structures face the highest risk during extreme weather events, and how does topography dictate local water pooling?

Methods & Steps: This environmental model was constructed by analyzing Digital Elevation Models (DEMs). I calculated hydrological flow accumulations, surface slope gradients, and pooling constraints in QGIS. By combining these topographic features with municipal asset layers, I mapped out high-vulnerability risk profiles.

Findings & Regional Patterns: While many expect coastal flooding to be the primary threat in the Netherlands, this model highlights a different risk. In areas like the dunes of The Hague, the coastal barrier remains highly resilient. Instead, flooding here is largely internal, driven by heavy rainfall over saturated soils and high groundwater tables that pool in low-lying urban areas behind the dunes.

Neuroscience Connection: Risk Cognition & Spatial Memory

How humans process environmental threats depends heavily on personal experience, a phenomenon tied to cognitive availability heuristics. Because historical flood barriers shape our spatial memories, internal water pooling represents an invisible risk that people often underestimate, as it isn't linked to a dramatic coastal threat.

Coursework Report: Vegetation Indices & Environmental Metrics

Course: Geographic Information Science and Systems (UC-SCI-EARL5) | Geosciences University College Utrecht
Instructor: Britta Ricker, PhD

This independent research project analyzes urban expansion and environmental health in Valencia, Spain, from 2017 to 2024. The following report integrates satellite remote sensing data with spatial theory to assess environmental sustainability.

1. True Color Imagery Baseline (2017)

This map establishes a 2017 snapshot of Valencia using a True Color Composite (RGB Bands 4, 3, 2) from Sentinel-2 satellite data. To generate this, I adapted JavaScript scripts within Google Earth Engine to aggregate raw, open satellite data captured during the dry summer season, filtering out cloud cover to output a clear orthomosaic.

An orthomosaic is a uniform mosaic image compiled from overlapping satellite photos. It is geometrically corrected to remove camera tilt and topographic distortions, making it accurate enough to measure true distances like a map.

Neuroscience Connection: Trichromatic Vision Alignment

True Color imagery directly matches the long (red), medium (green), and short (blue) wavelength sensitivities of human cone cells. This leverages existing neural networks for object recognition, allowing the brain to quickly classify buildings and landscapes based on lifelong color associations.

2. True Color Imagery Comparison (2024)

This map shows Valencia in 2024, using the exact same summer timeline and 10m/pixel spatial resolution settings as the 2017 baseline. Comparing the two images highlights clear changes along the city's edges, documenting urban expansion and land use shifts over a seven-year period.

Why seasonality matters: Choosing the same month is essential when building orthomosaics. Comparing a winter scene with a summer scene introduces seasonal changes in foliage and sun angles, creating false trends in our change analysis.

View Full Screen ↗

3. Baseline NDVI Spectrum Analysis (2017)

This map displays the calculated Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) for 2017, rendered using a Singleband Pseudocolor scale from -1 (unhealthy/water) to 1 (vibrant canopy). The calculation uses the formula:

NDVI = (B8 - B4) / (B8 + B4)

Where Band 8 is Near-Infrared (NIR) and Band 4 is Visible Red. This calculation works because healthy plant chlorophyll absorbs visible red light for photosynthesis but reflects near-infrared light back into space. By comparing these values, we can calculate a clear vegetation health score.

4. Comparative NDVI Spectrum Analysis (2024)

The 2024 NDVI layer maps out vegetation density across modern Valencia. This type of multispectral imagery is invaluable for urban planning. Because satellites record wavelengths beyond human vision, they allow us to monitor environmental changes that aren't visible from the ground.

This analytical approach directly supports UN Sustainable Development Goal 15 (Life on Land). It provides open, verifiable data to track urban expansion, protect surrounding ecosystems, and monitor city greening initiatives over time.

View Full Screen ↗

5. NDVI Environmental Change Delta Map

This final map shows net change over time, calculated via QGIS Raster Band Math:

NDVI 2017 - NDVI 2024 = Change Delta

Using a monochromatic scheme, this layout isolates environmental trends. Deep green shades represent biomass growth, while red tones reveal vegetation loss. This analysis helps differentiate actual urban expansion from temporary shifts caused by cloud artifacts or crop rotation cycles.

Supervised & Unsupervised Land Use / Land Cover (LULC) Classification

Course Requirement: Science Park Utrecht Analysis | Instructor: Britta Ricker, PhD

This module details how computers process satellite imagery, using machine learning classifiers to sort pixels and objects into land-cover maps. Below is the complete narrative answering all assignment evaluation criteria.

Esri Academic Certification

Course Completed: Performing Supervised Pixel-Based Image Classification (Esri 2026)

This certificate confirms my training in setting up training samples, managing multispectral bands, and calculating accuracy matrices using maximum likelihood and machine learning algorithms in ArcGIS Pro.

LULC Assignment Narrative Answers

Question 1: Automated Land Cover Value & Classification Paradigms

Automated land cover classification turns massive amounts of multi-band satellite imagery into categorized maps, allowing us to track global change at scale. The difference lies in where the instruction comes from: Supervised classification relies on user-defined training samples, where the cartographer manually identifies known sites (e.g., water, urban, forest) to build a statistical profile that guides the algorithm. In contrast, Unsupervised classification uses clustering algorithms (like Iso Cluster) to group pixels entirely by their mathematical similarities in light reflection, requiring the analyst to label the categories after the processing is complete.

Neuroscience Connection: Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up Processing

This division mirrors the brain's visual processing pathways. Unsupervised classification is like bottom-up processing—relying purely on raw sensory inputs and pixel frequencies to find patterns. Supervised classification behaves like top-down processing, where internal memories, expectations, and trained schemas guide our attention to categorize what we see.

Question 2: Spatial Resolution Metrics

Using the ArcGIS Pro measure tool across individual pixels within the Science Park satellite scene, the approximate resolution is 10 meters by 10 meters per pixel. This matches the standard spatial resolution of Sentinel-2's visible and near-infrared bands, meaning each pixel averages out data over a 100-square-meter footprint on the ground.

Question 3: Spectral Profile Diagnostics

Spectral profiles plot how different land types reflect light across multiple wavelengths, creating a unique signature for each surface. By selecting distinct training regions, I mapped signatures for four key classes: Water drops off sharply in the infrared bands because it absorbs long wavelengths, while Forest/Vegetation spikes clearly in the near-infrared spectrum. Urban surfaces show a steady, elevated reflection profile across all bands due to asphalt and concrete, whereas Open Fields display a distinct soil signature that sets them apart from dense canopies.

Question 5 & 6: Unsupervised Pixel-Based vs. Object-Based Segmentation

The pixel-based approach inspects every cell independently, often resulting in a messy "salt-and-pepper" effect where individual mismatched pixels disrupt large blocks of color. Object-based segmentation avoids this by grouping neighboring pixels with similar light profiles and shapes into cohesive objects before categorizing them. This approach creates a cleaner map that better aligns with real-world features.

Question 7 & 8: Supervised Outputs & Confusion Matrix Metrics

The supervised object-based approach yielded the cleanest alignment with real-world features. To assess its accuracy, I ran a Confusion Matrix, which compares the computer's classifications against real reference data.

This process evaluates two main types of error: Producer Error (omission) occurs when a real feature is left out of its correct category on the map. User Error (commission) occurs when a feature is incorrectly included in a category where it doesn't belong, misleading the map reader. My matrix revealed strong accuracy for water and forest, but showed slight confusion between urban concrete structures and bare soil fields.

Question 9: NDVI & Visual Communication Theory

The ideal color scheme for displaying NDVI uses a graded Red-Yellow-Green diverging ramp. This palette is highly effective for visual communication because humans have strong, culturally ingrained associations with these colors: red signals stress or non-vegetated terrain, yellow marks transition zones, and vibrant green indicates dense, healthy canopy. The primary target audience includes urban planners, environmental researchers, and local policymakers who need to assess ecosystem health quickly without decoding complex technical values.

Technological Limitations Notice

During our final lab session, unexpected software crashes and band-matching errors in the raster calculator prevented the system from rendering the final localized NDVI image layer for Science Park Utrecht. Rather than using broken files, this profile openly acknowledges those technical hurdles, while presenting the underlying logic and optimal color theory required for accurate environmental communication.

Question 10: Methodological Trade-offs & Data Anomalies

Each classification method has unique strengths and weaknesses:

  • Unsupervised Pixel: Fast and unbiased, but creates messy, fragmented maps that are difficult to interpret.
  • Unsupervised Object: Effectively groups distinct land features, but sometimes merges separate boundaries if they share similar colors.
  • Supervised Object-Based: Offers the highest overall accuracy by combining human knowledge with spatial shapes, though it requires significant time and manual effort to build clean training samples.

Anomalies in our dataset included strong reflections from metallic roofs at the Science Park, which the computer often misclassified as water bodies due to their unique light signatures across certain bands. This highlight shows why human oversight remains essential when auditing automated machine learning models.
