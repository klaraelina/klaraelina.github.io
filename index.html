Where the World Drinks Safely — SDG 6.1.1

This project involved creating a global choropleth map illustrating access to safely managed drinking water using the SDG 6.1.1 indicator. The workflow began in QGIS, where I joined national-level water access data to a country boundary shapefile, cleaned attribute fields, and applied graduated color classifications.

The interactive version was built using Leaflet.js, incorporating custom styling, dynamic pop-ups, and an interactive legend to display global inequalities cleanly.

Kindergarten Accessibility in Utrecht by Bicycle

Spatial Question: How accessible are local hospitals and kindergartens across different residential neighborhoods in Utrecht within a realistic cycling commute?

Methods & Process: Using vector spatial analysis tools, I generated localized service areas called "isochrones"—which map out polygons representing incremental travel thresholds (ranging from 4 to 24 minutes) along the actual bike path infrastructure of the municipality. Next, I used Vector -> Analysis -> Count Points in Polygon. Setting the isochrone layers as our boundary polygon and the location data as our target point features, QGIS tallied exactly how many assets fell within each travel zone.

Findings & Patterns: The resulting dataset clearly maps regional proximity profiles. Central Utrecht displays highly compact accessibility metrics, where residential clusters sit well within 4-to-8-minute thresholds. Moving toward suburban boundaries reveals distinct service gaps where families encounter longer travel paths, signaling uneven infrastructural distribution.

Flood Risk & Spatial Vulnerability Analysis

This spatial project models topographic exposure and localized catchment risks across the Netherlands. Built by evaluating hydrological overlay constraints, digital elevation structures, and flood plain boundaries, it uncovers spatial vulnerabilities across environmental and municipal assets.

The Dunes of The Hague: A surprising finding emerged when examining the coastal city of The Hague. While its coastal margins are protected by prominent, historically resilient sand dunes, the primary flooding hazard is actually internal rather than ocean-driven. Heavy rainfall forces the water table upward from beneath the low-lying interior soils, backing up urban canal networks and trapping water behind the very dunes meant to protect the coast.

The Brain on Greenery: A Neuroscientific Twist on Earth Observation

As a student of Cognitive Neuroscience and Chemistry, I look at satellite imagery through a unique lens. In neuroscience, Biophilia Hypothesis models show that viewing natural landscapes vs. urban concrete triggers clear physiological changes. Natural spaces reduce cortisol levels, lower sympathetic nervous system activation, and boost fronto-temporal alpha-wave activity associated with relaxed alertness.

By mapping vegetation indices in Valencia, Spain, we aren't just looking at environmental metrics. We are mapping the "cognitive health potential" of a city. This project demonstrates how satellite data tracks the balance between stress-inducing urban structures and brain-soothing natural ecosystems, supporting UN Sustainable Development Goal 15 (Life on Land).

Core Technical Framework

To evaluate Valencia's changing landscape, we must first understand the remote sensing data pipeline:

  • What is an orthomosaic? It is an aerial image compiled from a patchwork of overlapping satellite photographs. These individual frames are geometrically corrected for topographic relief, lens distortion, and camera tilt, transforming them into a mathematically uniform map with a reliable scale bar.
  • How do you make a cloudless composite? Using JavaScript in Google Earth Engine (GEE), I filtered thousands of Sentinel-2 data tiles over a specific date range. The algorithm screens each pixel, masks out cloud obstructions, and calculates the median value for every pixel coordinate across the time frame to produce a clean, cloudless image.
  • What is multispectral imagery? Unlike standard cameras that capture only visible True Color light (Red, Green, Blue), the Sentinel-2 satellite payload measures non-visible light wavelengths across 13 spectral bands. This includes Near-Infrared (NIR) and Shortwave Infrared (SWIR). This data is invaluable because different materials reflect unique spectral signatures. Healthy vegetation strongly absorbs visible red light but intensely reflects near-infrared light.
  • Why do date ranges and seasons matter? Phenology—the seasonal lifecycle of plants—fundamentally skews satellite calculations. Comparing an image taken during winter dormancy with one from summer peak bloom would yield misleading change data. For Valencia, I selected matching summer date ranges (June–August) for both 2017 and 2024 to keep solar angles and crop cycles consistent.

3. Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (Time 1: 2017)

What is NDVI and how does it work? The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index quantifies canopy health by calculating the ratio between near-infrared reflection and visible red absorption: (NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red). In QGIS Raster Calculator, this was coded using Sentinel-2 bands as: (B8 - B4) / (B8 + B4).

The index yields values from -1.0 to +1.0. Water bodies and urban pavement result in negative scores, while dense, healthy vegetation approaches +1.0. This 2017 map uses a Red-Yellow-Green gradient stretched strictly from -1 to 1, showing strong agricultural activity in the outer valleys.

5. Change Over Time Analysis Matrix Map

To pinpoint exactly where vegetation health shifted, I performed change detection math: NDVI 2017 - NDVI 2024 = Change Delta. The resulting map uses a monochromatic color scheme to highlight variations across the landscape.

Interpretation Warning: When evaluating change over time, some dramatic shifts are actually anomalies caused by minor atmospheric clouds or seasonal crop rotations rather than permanent urban expansion. However, this map successfully captures genuine urban growth patterns, tracking where natural habitats have been replaced by concrete development over the seven-year period.
